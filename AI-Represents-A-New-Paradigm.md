AI represents a new paradigm. However, many software design principles developed over the past 30 years are now more relevant than ever.
Software findimentals now matter more than ever
Dex Horley proposed an intriguing concept: the "Smart Zone" and "Dumb Zone" as a framework for understanding the peculiar constraints inherent in Large Language Models (LLMs). I want to  analogize that adding a token to an LLM chat with introducing a new team to a football league; each addition exponentially increases the potential number of matches, or interactions. This presentation will delve into these "Smart" and "Dumb" zones. The scaling of tokens in a chat is quadratic because each new token heightens the attention relationships, which are determined by both the token's position and its semantic connection to other tokens.
Personally I don't feel it matters if you are using a 200k or 1m context window, current models feel to get dumb after approximately 100k in tokens and therefore we should try and break up tasks appropriately. 
Drawing inspiration from Martin Fowler's advice in "The Pragmatic Programmer" on deconstructing complex tasks to prevent overwhelm, we can apply a similar principle to large language models. The challenge then becomes how to maintain these models within their "smart zone," preventing them from veering off course while optimizing their token utilization. This involves strategically managing input and output to ensure efficiency and accuracy, much like breaking down a software project into manageable components.
