We often encounter a significant amount of functional code that appears to operate flawlessly. As engineers, our initial reaction is typically one of satisfaction, marveling at its effectiveness. However, moments later, a different agent might be simultaneously generating exceptionally poor code in another terminal. This problematic output often goes unnoticed because we lack the immediate automation bias derived from our initial positive experience with the first terminal. It highlights how automation bias can sometimes prevent us from identifying when an agent, despite earlier successes, generates incorrect or substandard code.
When integrating new engineers, I habitually assess the caliber of their initial contributions. This evaluation then informs my decision on the level of oversight required for their subsequent assignments. In some instances, I may scrutinize an engineer's work for two to three months before deeming them ready for more autonomous tasks. However, a significant challenge arises with AI models: their performance lacks a quantifiable framework. They operate flawlessly until an unexpected failure occurs, and a mere three-month review period doesn't intrinsically foster trust. Consequently, adjustments to their operation can become necessary at any given moment.
