AI models particularly excel in industries and domains where verifiable outcomes are readily available, making it straightforward for a computer to validate data outputs. However, the efficacy of these models largely depends on the specific training data selected by development labs. Not all labs prioritize training models for every possible dataset; certain datasets hold more significance for a lab's success metrics. Generally, any area allowing for the verification of computations and outputs is where an AI model will perform exceptionally well.
A compelling anecdote from OpenAI illustrates this point: the significant improvement in chess-playing ability between ChatGPT 3.5 and GPT 4.0. Initially, many presumed this enhancement was a natural progression of the model becoming inherently smarter. Yet, it was later revealed that an individual at OpenAI deliberately incorporated chess data into the model's training set, directly leading to its improved performance. This demonstrates that intelligence can be specifically engineered through targeted data input, rather than being solely an emergent property of increased sophistication.
Conversely, models can exhibit unexpected limitations in other areas. An internet anecdote highlighted how a model, despite generating robust code and algorithmic interpretations, struggled with a simple query: counting the number of "r"s in "strawberry." A more recent example involves a query about traveling 25 meters to a car wash: "Should I walk or drive?" The model advised walking, deeming it quicker than the process of entering a car and fastening a seatbelt, thereby overlooking the fundamental need for a vehicle at a car wash.
These examples underscore that AI models are trained on datasets chosen by labs, often reflecting the labs' designed outcomes and customer base. While many jobs may involve verifiable outputs, their corresponding datasets might not be introduced to the models, leaving certain niche areas uncovered. This selective training means that a model's capabilities are largely shaped by the specific data it consumes, rather than an all-encompassing, generalized intelligence.
Expanding on this, the central inquiry shouldn't be about which jobs AI models can supplant, but rather which roles are most lucrative for laboratories to generate datasets. As previously stated, this is highly feasible. Any quantifiable outcome can be incentivized through reinforcement learning techniques with these models, leading to exceptional datasets and significantly enhanced performance. However, the profitability remains a key consideration. Thus, the pertinent question shifts from "Can AI perform my job?" to "Will research labs continue to advance and prioritize data set generation in that specific domain?" This presents an intriguing avenue for reflection and exploration.
Given this insight, if you are a business founder, your ideal specialization lies in a domain that frontier models are not actively seeking to dominate. This means avoiding areas like advanced math or coding, where these models are already highly proficient. Instead, focus on sectors with verifiable, deterministic outcomes where your reinforcement learning strategies can significantly enhance a model through fine-tuning, leveraging your preferred framework within a specific niche. This approach presents the greatest business potential with the least competition. For instance, developing a coding tool in 2026 might be a formidable challenge. However, creating a tool for a highly specialized data subset—one that existing models have limited training data for and are not prioritizing due to their core user base—is likely the optimal strategy. This represents a sweet spot for innovation and market entry.
